Income, well-being, and conflict

Across macroeconomic, health, and security literature, per-capita income appears as a robust predictor — sometimes as a control, sometimes as the focal predictor, but almost always in the expected direction. By pooling findings across three independently maintained paxes, an agent can answer a question that no single researcher needed to tackle.

The findings converge: higher income correlates with stability. The mechanism is debated (institutional capacity? literacy? consumption smoothing?), but the empirical pattern holds across methods and countries.

Climate stress on agricultural systems

Temperature anomalies appear in three distinct paxes — one focused on climate-conflict nexus, one on land-use change, one on air quality. Each pack studied temperature through a different lens. Pooling them surfaces a consistent finding: warming destabilizes agricultural yields, triggering downstream economic and political pressure.

The cross-pack view reveals nonlinearities that a single pack might miss: impact differs by latitude, soil type, and cropping system.

Compensation, mobility, and democratic erosion

Wage growth appears in labor-mobility studies, compensation research, and democratic-erosion literature. Each field has a hypothesis about its role. Pooling the findings reveals a paradox: regions with rising wages sometimes see *faster* democratic backsliding, contradicting the simple "prosperity → stability" narrative.

The resolution lies in heterogeneity: wage gains concentrate, leaving others behind. Relative deprivation — not absolute income — predicts backlash.
